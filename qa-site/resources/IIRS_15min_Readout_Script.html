Good morning. I am Gaurav Yadav. Our team is presenting IIRS, the Intelligent Incident Response System. It turns logs, metrics, traces, and runbook guidance into an Incident Brief with ranked causes, cited evidence, and safe next steps.

0:20[Advance to Slide 2] This is our seven-member team. You will hear from us in this order, with each person carrying the next part of the presentation.

0:35[Advance to Slide 3] Our contributions map to the system. I led integration, the end-to-end workflow, Retriever, evidence schema, queries, and the incident scenario. Vamsi handled integration, telemetry instrumentation, scenario design, and dataset export. Rashmi built Analyst hypothesis ranking, confidence scoring, and next checks. Avinash built Critic verification, hallucination defences, and approval gating. Dinesh added Critic safety checks, failure-mode tests, chat UX, and approval documentation. Krishna built Planner orchestration, tool calling, whitelist enforcement, and audit logging. Pavan built chat, follow-up support, and the README.

1:10[Advance to Slide 4] We will define the incident-response problem, then show our objectives, architecture, workflow, and technology choices. Finally, we will compare a run with no confirmed fault against a likely service failure.

1:30[Advance to Slide 5] The problem is not a lack of telemetry. Distributed applications already produce huge volumes of it. The problem is correlation. During an incident, an on-call engineer must move between metrics, logs, and traces; align the time window; connect symptoms across services; consult a runbook; and decide what is safe to do. Under pressure, that manual process is slow and inconsistent, especially for an L1 responder.

2:00IIRS automates that first investigation, but not the fix. We set four trust requirements. First, evidence-grounded: diagnoses cite supporting telemetry or knowledge. Second, auditable: each stage and tool call is recorded in a JSON trace. Third, safe by design: diagnostic checks are read-only by default. Fourth, human in the loop: restarts, rollbacks, failovers, reconfiguration, and traffic changes remain approval-gated recommendations.

2:35Our central claim is modest and testable: IIRS assembles the first evidence-backed diagnosis while leaving judgment and control with the on-call engineer.

Rashmi — System Architecture

Slide 7 · four stages with explicit contracts

2:15

Thank you, Vamsi. [Advance to Slide 7] IIRS is a fixed four-stage pipeline: Retriever, Analyst, Critic, and Planner. Calling out the boundaries matters because not every stage should use generative AI.

0:20The Retriever is deterministic and tooling-based. It queries Loki for logs, Prometheus for metrics, Tempo for traces, and relevant local runbooks. It packages every result into an Evidence Bundle with a unique ID. Keeping retrieval deterministic makes the evidence foundation reproducible.

0:50The Analyst is LLM-backed. It reads that bundle and produces ranked root-cause hypotheses. Each hypothesis includes a confidence score, supporting and contradicting evidence IDs, and next checks. The prompt requires evidence references, but that instruction alone is not enough.

1:20So the Critic, also LLM-backed, reviews the same evidence and challenges the Analyst. It flags weak citations, alternative explanations, missing data, and unsafe assumptions. Its role is not to guarantee that hallucination is impossible; its role is to expose unsupported reasoning before it reaches the operator.

1:50Finally, the Planner converts the reviewed state into an Incident Brief. It places read-only diagnostic steps under auto-safe and any mutating action under needs-approval. LangGraph passes a shared structured state through this fixed sequence, and a local fallback runner preserves the same stages when LangGraph is unavailable.

2:05That architecture explains who does what.

Flex line — skip only if more than 10 seconds late

The fixed sequence is an MVP trade-off: it is predictable and testable, but it does not yet loop back to collect new evidence when the Critic finds a gap.

Avinash — End-to-End Workflow

Slide 8 · from application signal to auditable brief

2:00

Thank you, Rashmi. [Advance to Slide 8] This slide shows eight steps, which I will group into collection, reasoning, and delivery.

0:15In the collection phase, Aspire Shop generates telemetry. It is a .NET microservices application with a frontend, catalog and basket services, PostgreSQL, and Redis. The services emit OTLP data to the OpenTelemetry Collector. The Collector exports logs to Loki, metrics for Prometheus to scrape, and traces to Tempo.

0:50When an engineer submits an alert or a plain-English incident prompt, the Python backend parses the service, environment, summary, and time window. The Retriever then asks only the parameterised queries relevant to that incident. The results become structured evidence items such as a connection-refused log, an up-status metric, a failed span, or a runbook excerpt.

1:20In the reasoning phase, the Analyst, Critic, and Planner receive that structured state in sequence. They rank hypotheses, test the evidence chain, and separate diagnostic checks from approval-required remediation.

1:40In the delivery phase, Chainlit renders stage progress and the final Incident Brief. The same pipeline can run from the iirs command line for reproducibility. Every completed run also writes a JSON trace containing the alert, evidence, agent outputs, execution modes, and final brief.

After the brief, follow-up questions such as “why?” or “what should I do first?” are answered from the saved incident state without running retrieval again.

Slide 9 · one reason for each technology choice

1:45

Thank you, Avinash. [Advance to Slide 9] This stack is intentionally conventional because the project risk is in evidence correlation and safe reasoning, not in inventing infrastructure.

1:30The important point is that each technology has one clear responsibility.

Viva Q&A — quick answers

Question round only. Do NOT read this during the 15-minute presentation.

Slide says FastAPI — where exactly is FastAPI used?

Through Chainlit's server stack. Our repository does not define a separate FastAPI application or custom REST endpoints. The second entry point is the iirs CLI, which invokes the same pipeline without the web UI.

Why is the pipeline linear? Why no loops between agents?

Deliberate. Linear means predictable, auditable, and easy to test. The Critic already gives in-pipeline feedback without loops. Loops add non-termination risk.

Why is the Retriever deterministic — isn't that a limitation?

Yes, it is both a safety choice and a limitation. Parameterised queries make retrieval reproducible and prevent invented queries, but the Retriever cannot dynamically decide that a novel signal is needed. Adaptive retrieval is future work.

What stops the LLM from hallucinating a root cause?

Nothing makes hallucination impossible. We reduce the risk with structured evidence IDs, prompts that require supporting and contradicting citations, a separate Critic pass, strict output schemas, approval gating, and regression evals. The operator can inspect the trace instead of trusting prose alone.

What if Prometheus or Loki is down? What if the LLM call fails?

Missing or unreachable telemetry raises TelemetryConfigurationError or TelemetryRequestError. A model timeout or invalid structured output raises OpenAIRequestError. Model-backed runs stop instead of silently replacing the answer with deterministic reasoning.

Can IIRS catch a dependency failure — say Postgres down but the alert is on catalog service?

Yes — that is exactly what the trace and metric evidence enables. Catalog service shows timeouts, but Tempo traces and the Postgres up-metric point to the database as the real cause. Our fault-injection script can stop Postgres or Redis to reproduce this.

Difference between iirs eval and iirs verify-live?

eval is an offline regression harness over fixed routing and pipeline cases. The current 46/46 result was run with OpenAI disabled, so it proves deterministic baseline behaviour, not model accuracy. verify-live separately checks that the PLT backends contain expected PostgreSQL or Redis fault signals.

Why OpenAI and not a local model?

We chose GPT-5 mini for reliable structured output and reasoning within the project schedule. Model access is isolated in src/iirs/llm.py, and the base URL can target an OpenAI-compatible endpoint. A non-compatible provider would still require adapter work and fresh evaluation.

Are the 0.70 and 0.87 confidence scores calibrated probabilities?

No. They are model or pipeline confidence values used to rank hypotheses within a run. We do not yet have enough labelled incidents to claim statistical calibration. We judge the current prototype through ranked outcomes, citations, safety checks, and fixed-case regressions.

Does the 46/46 eval prove the AI works in production?

No. It proves that nine fixed cases currently satisfy forty-six explicit checks. That is useful regression evidence, but the corpus is small and the latest baseline is deterministic. Broader labelled incidents and model-enabled evaluation are required before production claims.

Were the two outputs shown on Slides 10 and 11 actually model-backed?

Yes. Their matching JSON traces record the Retriever as tooling and the Analyst, Critic, and Planner as model. Keep that separate from the 46/46 offline eval result, which is the deterministic regression baseline.

Do the JSON traces contain the model's private chain of thought?

No. They contain the structured, auditable artifacts we need: evidence, hypotheses, critique findings, action plan, execution modes, timestamps, tool calls, and summaries. We do not claim to store hidden chain-of-thought reasoning.

What exactly is live in the demonstration?

Aspire Shop and the local PLT stack can run live, and evidence IDs with .live. indicate the PLT backend path. The submitted slides show captured runs. For PostgreSQL and Redis, iirs verify-live validates expected signals separately; it does not validate the quality of the LLM diagnosis.

What are the most important current limitations?

Small eval corpus, deterministic template-based retrieval, only two live fault-signature profiles, no continuous monitoring loop, no autonomous executor, and an externally fetched Aspire Shop dependency. These boundaries are deliberate and documented.
